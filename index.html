The Narrative

Three regimes. Three decisions. One sourcing reality.

01 · THE INFLECTION

The 2018 Section 301 tariffs created a structural break, not a level shift.

Before mid-2018, US imports of electric motors, generators, and insulated wire/cable from China were growing at +$469M per year (R² = 0.98). After Section 301, that slope flipped to -$159M per year — a $628M/year trend reversal that has persisted for seven consecutive years.

The segmented regression isolates this as a true direction change, not a one-time level adjustment. The difference-in-differences model — treating China as the treated unit against {Mexico, Vietnam, India} as controls — estimates that China imports are 63% below the peer-supplier trajectory in the post-2018 era (log-USD coefficient -0.984, R² = 0.96). The break is causal, not coincidental.

02 · THE REDISTRIBUTION

Mexico absorbed the volume. Concentration didn't fall — it relocated.

Mexico imports grew from $12.5B (2016) to $22.0B (2025) — a 76% gain with a slope of $1.08B/year (R² = 0.88, p < 0.0001). USMCA implementation in 2020 reinforced a trend that had already begun. Mexico now commands 45% of these imports, structurally similar to where the US sat with China in 2008–2010.

Six China-alternative suppliers also expanded sharply: Vietnam +462%, India +682%, Malaysia +569%, Thailand +271%, Indonesia +203%, Philippines +86%. All slopes statistically significant. But collectively they remain less than 18% of the 2025 mix — meaningful diversification has not yet been achieved.

03 · THE EQUILIBRIUM

The US is past the inflection but not through the diversification.

Three overlapping game-theoretic equilibria explain why the system landed here. The US-China tariff dynamic is a Prisoner's Dilemma stuck in mutual defection. The intra-industry coordination problem — should I nearshore while my competitor stays in China? — is a second Prisoner's Dilemma in which tariffs act as a coordination device, pushing firms from the (Stay, Stay) Nash to the (Nearshore, Nearshore) cooperative outcome. Escalation events approach a Chicken Game whose catastrophic decoupling outcome remains low-probability per cycle but cumulatively non-trivial.

For procurement, this means the historical post-2018 slope is the planning baseline. Forecasts built on the full-period regression underestimate the structural shift. The composite supplier-risk score quantifies where to act next — and HHI alone is no longer enough.

Seven operational recommendations a sourcing director can deploy this quarter.

Build Chicken-game optionality
Dual-source critical SKUs between Tier-1 (Mexico, Vietnam) and Tier-2 (India, Thailand, Indonesia). The marginal cost is small vs. the expected loss in a decoupling scenario.
